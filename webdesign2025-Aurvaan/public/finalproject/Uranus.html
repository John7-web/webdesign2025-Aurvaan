Uranus — In-depth Overview

Uranus is an ice giant and the seventh planet from the Sun, known for its extreme axial tilt and faint ring system. This section summarizes Uranus’ formation, interior, atmosphere, rings, moons, magnetosphere, and exploration history.

Formation & Evolution

Uranus formed about 4.5 billion years ago in the outer Solar System. Its composition of ices and gases suggests accretion of volatile-rich planetesimals, and a giant impact early in its history likely produced its extreme axial tilt (~98°).

Internal Structure

Uranus likely has a rocky core, an icy mantle composed of water, ammonia, and methane ices, and an outer envelope of hydrogen and helium. The 'ice' mantle may behave fluidly under high pressure and temperature conditions.

Atmosphere & Weather

Uranus’ atmosphere contains hydrogen, helium, and methane (which gives the blue-green color). The planet shows banded clouds, seasonal changes due to its tilt, and occasionally visible storms revealed by Hubble and ground-based observations.

Rings & Moons

Uranus has a system of faint rings and 27 known moons, including Titania, Oberon, Ariel, and Miranda, which display diverse geology such as extensional faults and resurfaced terrain.

Magnetosphere

Uranus’ magnetic field is significantly tilted and offset from its rotational axis, creating complex interactions with the solar wind and contributing to auroral phenomena.

Exploration & Science

Voyager 2 flew past Uranus in 1986, providing most of our close-up knowledge. Future missions and telescopic observations aim to better characterize its interior, atmosphere, rings, and satellites.

Quick Facts

  • Mean radius: ~25,362 km
  • Mass: ~8.68 × 1025 kg
  • Rotation period: ~17.2 hours
  • Main atmospheric gases: H₂, He, with methane traces
  • Number of known moons: 27

Sources: Voyager 2, Hubble Space Telescope, NASA planetary research articles.
